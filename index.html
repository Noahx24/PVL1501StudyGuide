Unit 02

Introduction to the Law of Persons

The law of persons is the very first branch of private law you study — and there's a reason for that. Law exists to govern relationships between people. Before you can understand anything else (contracts, property, marriage, crime), you need to understand who the law recognises as a "person" in the first place.

What is the Law of Persons?

The law of persons is the part of private law that asks four questions about legal subjects:

  • What kinds of legal subjects exist?
  • How does a legal subject begin and end?
  • What does legal status involve?
  • What factors affect a person's status?

Legal Subject vs Legal Object

Plain English Think of it like a game. The players are legal subjects — they score, foul, win, lose. The ball, field, equipment are legal objects — used by players but can't play themselves.
Legal subject
Any entity the law recognises as able to have rights, duties and capacities.
Legal object
Anything a legal subject can have rights in — but which cannot itself have rights. (A car, a house, an invention, your good name.)
Legal capacity
The ability to have rights and duties.

Two Kinds of Legal Subjects

LEGAL SUBJECTS (Persons in Law)
Natural persons Every human being — regardless of age, ability, or mental capacity.
Juristic (artificial) persons Associations the law treats as persons in their own right, separate from their members.

All humans are legal subjects. Not all legal subjects are humans.

Juristic persons recognised in SA law

  1. Associations incorporated under general legislation (companies, banks).
  2. Associations specifically created by separate legislation (universities, Eskom, SABC).
  3. Associations meeting common-law requirements (universitates).
Tjollo Ateljees (Eins) Bpk v Small
1949 (1) SA 856 (A)
IssueWhether the old Roman/Roman-Dutch doctrine of the monstrum (severely malformed child who could be killed with a magistrate's permission) still formed part of South African law.
HeldThe court rejected the doctrine outright, stating that a "monstrum" in the Roman sense is a physical impossibility.
Why it matters: Confirms that every human being in modern SA law is a legal subject — no exception for disability or malformation.

  4. No — a partnership is not a juristic person. The partners carry the rights and duties personally.

Transformative Constitutionalism

The Constitution doesn't just set rules — it commits the country to changing society itself. "Transformative constitutionalism" means using law to move from an unjust past to a society based on dignity, equality and freedom. You'll see this theme throughout the law of persons.

  • Living customary law — how communities actually practise customary law today.

Unit 03

The Beginning of Legal Personality

Before you are born, you are not a legal subject. Legal personality begins at birth — but what exactly counts as "birth" in law?

The Two Requirements

Mother is pregnant — foetus is not yet a legal subject
↓
Requirement 1: Birth must be fully completed (body of mother and body of child completely separated). The umbilical cord does NOT need to be cut.
↓
Requirement 2: The child must live after separation — even for a short time. (The "born-alive rule".)
↓
LEGAL PERSONALITY BEGINS ✓

Both requirements must be met. A stillborn child never acquires legal personality.

Remember "Completely separated + any sign of life = a legal person is born."

How is "life after birth" proved?

SA law has not authoritatively decided. Evidence that the child breathed is normally accepted, but any medical evidence is admissible.

What about viability?

Some older authors said the child must also be "viable" (able to exist independently of the mother's body). Heaton rejects this — viability is a vague concept that leads to impossible problems of evidence. Viability is NOT a requirement in SA law.

Feedback
  1. At birth.
  2. (a) The birth must be fully completed — the bodies must be completely separated; (b) the child must live after separation, even briefly.
  3. False. Separation means the bodies are apart — the umbilical cord does not need to be cut for birth to be legally complete.

Registration of Births — Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992

Unit 04

Interests of the Unborn Child (Nasciturus)

Unit 3 said legal personality only begins at birth. But what if something happens before the child is born that would benefit them after birth? This is where the nasciturus fiction steps in.

What is the Nasciturus Fiction?

Nasciturus
A conceived but unborn child (a foetus).
Fiction
Something the law pretends is true even though it isn't.
The nasciturus fiction
Whenever it would be to the advantage of the unborn child, the law treats them as if they had already been born at the time of conception.
In abeyance
"Held aside" — the rights are parked until we know whether the baby will be born alive.

The Two Requirements

1. The child must have been conceived when the benefit would have accrued.
+
2. The child must be subsequently born alive.
↓
Fiction applies — benefit goes to the child

The "must benefit" rule

The fiction only operates if applying it would benefit the nasciturus. It can still apply where both the child and a third party benefit — but not where only third parties would benefit.

Activity 4.1 — Requirements of the fiction
  1. Fill in the missing words: "The law protects the unborn child's ________ ________ by pretending the nasciturus was born at the time of ________. The position is kept in ________ until birth."
  2. List the two requirements for the fiction to apply.
  3. Give an example where the fiction would benefit only third parties.
  4. Give an example where the fiction benefits both the nasciturus and a third party.
Feedback
  1. potential interests, conception, abeyance.
  2. (a) Conceived at the time the benefit would have accrued; (b) subsequently born alive.
  3. The unborn child was entitled to inherit, but dies shortly after birth. Only the child's own intestate heirs (not the child) would benefit — so the fiction does not apply.
  4. Grandpa leaves money to the unborn child; the child is born alive and inherits. Both the child (directly) and the parents (because the child can now support themselves, reducing maintenance burden) benefit.

Where the Fiction Applies

4.3.1 Patrimonial Interests — Succession

Ex parte Boedel Steenkamp
1962 (3) SA 954 (O)
FactsA testator left the residue of his estate to his daughter and her children "who are alive at the time of my death". When he died, the daughter had two children (D and G) already born, and was pregnant with P, who was later born alive.
IssueCould P (the unborn child at the time of death) share in the inheritance along with D and G?
HeldYes. Judge De Villiers applied the nasciturus fiction: a conceived child is presumed alive for purposes of succession provided the child is later born alive and the fiction benefits the child. P was included in the inheritance.
NoteHeaton criticises this decision because the will's wording arguably excluded the unborn child — but the court held the testator's intention was not unambiguously exclusive.
Why it matters: Classic application of the fiction in testate succession — the leading SA case on the point.
Activity 4.2 — Testate succession

  2. C was conceived when the testator died, and born alive afterwards. In each clause below, does C inherit?
    • (a) "I leave my estate to A, B and D."
    • (b) "I leave my estate to my daughter's children, born or still to be born."

Feedback
  1. Testate succession: the deceased left a valid will. Intestate succession: the deceased left no will — distribution follows the Intestate Succession Act.
  2. (a) No — the testator named specific beneficiaries; C is not mentioned. (b) Yes — the testator's intention clearly includes the unborn. (c) Yes (Ex parte Boedel Steenkamp) — applying the nasciturus fiction, C is deemed alive at the testator's death.

4.3.1 Patrimonial Interests — Maintenance after birth

Shields v Shields
1946 CPD 242
FactsA pregnant woman divorced the father of her unborn child. They entered a settlement agreement stating the father would NOT be liable for the child's maintenance after birth, and asked the court to make it part of the divorce order.
HeldThe court refused. A mother cannot waive her unborn child's right to claim maintenance — such an agreement is contra bonos mores (against good morals / the community's legal convictions).
Why it matters: The unborn child's future right to maintenance cannot be bargained away by either parent.

4.3.1 Patrimonial Interests — Dependant's action for loss of support

Chisholm v East Rand Proprietary Mines Ltd
1909 TH 297
FactsA child's father was killed by another person's delict before the child was born. The child, once born alive, sued for loss of support.
HeldThe child can recover. This was the first SA case applying the nasciturus fiction in the law of delict.
Why it matters: Opened the door for delictual claims where the wrongful act happens before the child's birth.

4.3.2 Prenatal Injury

Pinchin v Santam Insurance Co Ltd
1963 (2) SA 254 (W)
FactsA pregnant woman was injured in a car accident. Her child was born with cerebral palsy. The father alleged the brain injuries were caused by the negligent driver.
IssueCan a person sue for injuries inflicted while still a foetus?
HeldJudge Hiemstra ruled that in principle a child has an action for prenatal injury — but applied the nasciturus fiction as the basis. On the facts, the action still failed because brain damage was not proved to be caused by the accident.
Why it matters: First SA case to recognise prenatal injury claims — though its reliance on the nasciturus fiction was later criticised.
Road Accident Fund v Mtati ★ PRESCRIBED CASE
2005 (6) SA 215 (SCA)
FactsA pregnant woman was knocked down and seriously injured by a negligent driver. Her child Z was later born with brain injuries and a mental disability. Z's father, as natural guardian, sued the Road Accident Fund on Z's behalf.
RAF's special plea(1) An unborn child is not a person and cannot claim compensation; (2) a driver owes no duty of care to an unborn child.
Legal questionDoes Z have a claim for prenatal injuries? Should it be based on the nasciturus fiction or on the ordinary law of delict?
HeldThe SCA dismissed the RAF's appeal. The child does have a claim, but based on the ordinary principles of the law of delict — NOT the nasciturus fiction. The delictual right of action becomes "complete" when the child is born alive. Elements of a delict (wrongfulness, damage) don't have to arise simultaneously.
ReasoningIt would be "intolerable" if our law gave no action for prenatal injuries. Using the fiction was unnecessary and undesirable in delict.
Why it matters (exam critical): Settled the debate. All future prenatal injury claims → ordinary law of delict. The nasciturus fiction still applies elsewhere (e.g. succession).

Christian League of Southern Africa v Rall
1981 (2) SA 821 (O)
FactsAn organisation applied for the appointment of a curator ad litem (a legal guardian for litigation purposes) to represent the interests of a foetus in matters concerning a proposed termination of pregnancy.
HeldApplication dismissed. A child has no rights that can be enforced on its behalf before being born alive — because legal personality begins at birth. The court also expressed the view that the protection of a foetus is based on a fiction.
Why it matters: Pre-constitutional authority that a foetus is not a legal subject with enforceable rights.
Christian Lawyers Association of SA v Minister of Health (No 1)
1998 (4) SA 1113 (T)
FactsThe plaintiffs argued that human life begins at conception and that the Choice on Termination of Pregnancy Act is unconstitutional because it contravenes section 11 (right to life).
HeldMcCreath J rejected the argument. No provision of the Constitution bestows legal personality or protection on the foetus. The Constitution does not qualify a woman's right to bodily autonomy to protect a foetus.
Why it matters: Confirmed the foetus has no constitutional right to life enforceable against the pregnant woman's right to choose.
Christian Lawyers Association of SA v Minister of Health (No 2)
2005 (1) SA 509 (T) — "Reproductive Health Alliance as amicus"
FactsChallenge to the provisions allowing a minor to consent independently to termination of her own pregnancy (without parental consent).
HeldThe court upheld the provisions. The cornerstone of the Act is informed consent. If the minor has the maturity to give informed consent, she may choose. The minor must be advised to consult parents/guardian, but the termination cannot be denied if she refuses.
Why it matters: Even a pregnant minor decides alone — parental consent is not required for termination of pregnancy under the CTOP Act.
Friedman v Glicksman
1996 (1) SA 1134 (W)
HeldGoldblatt J indicated that the nasciturus fiction is NOT the basis for a wrongful life action. The child's action arises not when the pregnancy is not terminated, but when the child is actually born.
Why it matters: Supports the view that the nasciturus fiction is unnecessary — ordinary delict principles suffice.

  • Minors: May only be sterilised if life would be jeopardised or seriously impaired otherwise.

Unit 05

The End of Legal Personality

Legal personality ends at death. But death isn't always simple — sometimes a person just disappears, and the law needs a way to deal with that uncertainty.

When is Someone Legally Dead?

Before the National Health Act 61 of 2003, SA had no legal definition of death. The Act introduced brain death as the criterion — but only for specific purposes (health services, organ donation, post-mortems). For other purposes, courts rely on medical evidence.

Activity 5.1 — Defining death

What criteria are applied to determine when a person is legally dead?

Feedback

Prior to the National Health Act 61 of 2003, no legal definition existed. The Act adopted brain death for the specific matters it governs (organ/tissue donation, post-mortems, etc.). In other contexts, courts rely on medical evidence as before.

Ex parte Estate Russell
1926 WLD 118
FactsApplication for a presumption-of-death order for a missing person.
HeldThe court laid down the key factors to consider: (1) the age of the missing person; (2) the length of absence; (3) whether the person was engaged in a dangerous trade/occupation; (4) general circumstances. In the absence of evidence of dangerous occupation, mere long absence is not enough.
Why it matters: Leading authority on the factors a court considers in a common-law presumption-of-death application. No fixed period applies in SA law — 30 years has been refused; shorter periods have sufficed when probability of death is very high.

Effect of a Presumption of Death Order

  • The estate can be administered as if the person were dead.
  • The presumption is rebuttable — can be set aside if further evidence shows the person is alive.
  • If the person reappears, they can sue those enriched at their expense for return of property.
  • If the court refuses, it may still order the estate to be divided among heirs — who must give security for return.
Activity 5.5 — After the order

  2. What if the missing person turns out to be alive?
Feedback
  1. Yes — the estate is administered in the normal way, as if death were proved.
  2. The presumption is rebuttable. The court that made the order can set it aside. The missing person can sue for enrichment against anyone unjustly enriched at their expense.
Activity 5.6 — Remarriage

  1. Under what Act does she apply to dissolve her marriage?
  2. Is a separate application necessary?
  3. What is the effect of the dissolution order?
Feedback

  2. Yes, in the common-law procedure. The court is not bound to grant the order but has a discretion.

Activity 5.7 — Statutory procedure

A presumption of death was expressed under the Inquests Act. Is a separate application to dissolve the marriage necessary?

Feedback

No. Section 2 of the Dissolution of Marriages on Presumption of Death Act provides that a presumption under the Inquests Act, once confirmed by the High Court, automatically dissolves the marriage/civil union.

Activity 5.8 — Refusal but division

Mrs Jonas's application was NOT successful. May the estate still be divided among heirs?

Feedback

Yes. Even if the court refuses to express a presumption, it may still order the missing person's property divided among heirs, typically subject to the heirs giving sufficient security for its return if the missing person reappears.

Sequence of Death (Commorientes)

When several people die in the same disaster (plane crash, car accident), determining who died first matters for inheritance. SA law has no presumption — the party alleging a sequence must prove it on a balance of probabilities.

Registration of Deaths & Duty to Bury

  • Every death must be reported to the Director-General of Home Affairs.
  • A burial order must be issued before burial or cremation.
  • The deceased's expressed wishes about funeral/burial generally take priority; the heir has the duty to bury.
Activity 5.9 — Conflicting burial wishes

Thomas died. On his deathbed he told his girlfriend he wanted to be cremated and his ashes scattered on his birth farm. Two years before, his will said he should be buried next to his father in the family graveyard. In his will he named his mother as sole heir. Girlfriend wants cremation; mother wants burial. What happens?

Feedback

The deceased's expressed wishes regarding burial are generally respected. The will is the most reliable written expression of his wishes. The more recent informal deathbed statement may be considered, but courts generally prefer the clearer, written directive. The heir (the mother) has the legal duty to bury and practical authority — she should act in accordance with Thomas's wishes as expressed in the will, meaning burial in the family graveyard. Courts balance practical considerations, fairness and family relationships.

Unit 06

Status — Defining Key Concepts

We now move from whether someone is a legal subject to what kind of legal subject they are. This is the world of "status" — the attributes affecting what a person can and cannot do in law.

What is Status?

Status comes from the Latin stare — "to stand". It is a person's "standing" in the law. Main status-affecting factors in private law: domicile, birth of unmarried parents, youth, physical or mental illness/incapacity, intoxication, prodigality, insolvency.

Customary Law Note In customary law, "status" refers to social standing, not legal standing. A first wife in a polygynous marriage has higher social status than later wives — but her legal status is the same.
Bhe v Magistrate, Khayelitsha (Commission for Gender Equality as Amicus Curiae)
2005 (1) SA 580 (CC)
IssueConstitutionality of the customary-law rule of male primogeniture (only the eldest male heir inherits).
HeldThe Constitutional Court declared the rule unconstitutional because it discriminated against women and against children not born first.
Why it matters: Landmark decision dismantling gender-based inequality in customary law of succession.

The Four Capacities

CAPACITIES A LEGAL SUBJECT CAN HAVE
1. Legal capacityTo have rights and duties. All humans have this. Even a baby, an insolvent, a mentally ill person.
2. Capacity to actTo perform valid juristic acts (e.g. sign a contract, make a will). Limited or absent for some.
3. Capacity to litigateTo appear in court as a party. Usually mirrors capacity to act.
4. AccountabilityTo be held liable for crimes and delicts. Requires ability to distinguish right from wrong.

Critical Distinction

Legal capacity (the capacity to have rights) is not the same as the rights you actually have right now. A baby has legal capacity — they can own property — even if they don't own any yet.

A 5-year-old child:
✅ Has legal capacity (can inherit, own toys, be a crime victim)
❌ Has no capacity to act (cannot sign a valid contract)
❌ Has no capacity to litigate (parent acts for them)
❌ Has no accountability (cannot be held criminally or delictually liable on the basis of fault)

Why Age / Mental Capacity Affects Status

To perform a valid juristic act, you need reasonable understanding and judgement — the ability to grasp the nature, purport and consequences of your acts. Persons under 18 are assumed not yet to have this fully, so their capacities are limited to protect them.

  3. The capacity to have rights and duties — held by all humans.

  5. The capacity to appear in court as a litigating party. Closely linked to capacity to act.
  6. The capacity to be at fault — to be held liable for crimes and delicts. Requires ability to tell right from wrong and act accordingly. Influenced by age and mental capacity.

Unit 07

Domicile

In SA, your lex domicilii — the law of where you are domiciled — determines your status. So domicile matters hugely. And it's not the same as where you live.

Why Domicile Matters

Your lex domicilii determines:

  • Whether you're a minor
  • Whether you were born of married or unmarried parents (domicile at birth = domicile of origin)
  • Intestate succession of your movable property
  • Your capacity to make a will for movable property
  • The matrimonial property system of your marriage (husband's domicile at marriage decides)
  • Court jurisdiction over matters of your status

Definition of Domicile

Domicile is the place where a person is deemed to be constantly present:

  • for the purpose of exercising rights,
  • and fulfilling obligations,
  • even in the event of factual absence.

Activity 7.1 — Components of domicile

List the different components of the definition of domicile.

Feedback

(1) The place where the person is deemed constantly present; (2) for exercising rights; (3) and fulfilling obligations; (4) even when factually absent.

General Principles

  1. Every person must have a domicile at all times — the law doesn't allow status to be undetermined.
  2. A change of domicile is never accepted without proof — once established at a place, that domicile persists until proven otherwise.
  3. No-one can have more than one domicile at a time.

Governing law: The Domicile Act 3 of 1992 replaced the common-law rules, in force from 1 August 1992 (not retrospective).

The Legal Status of a Minor — General Principles

Between 7 and 18, a child is a minor with limited capacity to act. Old enough to think — not yet, in law's view, old enough to be trusted alone with binding agreements.

The Golden Rule

A minor (7–18) cannot incur contractual liability unless they are assisted by their guardian when the contract is concluded.

Three Types of Contracts a Minor Might Enter

A MINOR'S CONTRACT
1. Pure benefit (rights only, no duties)✅ VALID WITHOUT ASSISTANCEExample: accepting a donation, being released from a debt.
2. Mixed (rights AND duties)⚠️ LIMPING CONTRACT without assistance — only the other party is bound; the minor is not (unless the guardian ratifies).
3. Pure burden (only duties)❌ INVALID without assistance — minor cannot only assume duties.

The "Limping" Unassisted Contract

When a minor enters an unassisted contract imposing duties:

  1. The other party is fully bound. They can't escape by pointing to the minor's minority.
  2. The minor is NOT bound — at least not yet.
  3. The guardian decides:
    • Ratify (approve) → contract becomes fully binding on the minor.
    • Reject → the other party loses rights against the minor.
16-year-old Sipho buys a laptop on credit without telling his mother.
• The shop is fully bound — must deliver.
• Sipho is not bound — can refuse to pay.
• Sipho's mother (guardian) decides: ratify (Sipho must pay) or reject (deal collapses; shop gets laptop back).

The Exceptio Non Adimpleti Contractus

In reciprocal contracts, if one party hasn't performed, the other can refuse to perform until they do. For a minor, this means the minor can demand performance from the other party without being obliged to perform themselves (unless the guardian ratifies).

Forms Guardian's Assistance Can Take

  1. Prior consent — express or implied, before the contract.
  2. Present at conclusion — guardian consents on the spot.
  3. Guardian concludes on minor's behalf — as representative.
  4. Ratification after the fact — guardian approves afterwards.
  5. Court / Master consent — for transactions above statutory thresholds (e.g. minor's immovable property).

Impact of the Consumer Protection Act (CPA)

Heaton notes that the CPA may cut across the traditional rules — an unassisted minor's consumer contract may be void (not merely limping). This area is developing; the CPA can invalidate consumer contracts with unassisted minors altogether.

LEARNING UNIT 13

The Minor's Contractual Capacity — Assistance by the Guardian, Ratification & Statutory Exceptions

You already know from Unit 12 that a minor who contracts without a guardian's assistance cannot be held contractually liable. This unit covers what happens when the minor does have assistance, or when the contract is ratified afterwards.

13.1 Assistance by the Guardian

A parent or guardian may assist a minor in contracting in three ways:

  1. The guardian enters into the contract on behalf of the minor (acting as the minor's representative).
  2. The minor enters the contract personally, but with the consent of the guardian (express or tacit).
  3. The guardian ratifies the contract after it has already been concluded (see 13.3 below).
KEY PRINCIPLE

When a minor has proper guardian assistance, the minor is liable ex contractu (on the contract) as if he or she were a major. The assistance effectively removes the limitation on the minor's capacity for that contract.

Contracts a Guardian CANNOT Conclude on the Minor's Behalf

There are limits to what the guardian may do for the minor:

  • Contracts of a closely personal nature — e.g. an antenuptial contract (the minor themselves must enter it, though the guardian may consent).
  • Contracts that only come into operation when the minor reaches majority.
  • Heaton submits that a guardian cannot conclude a contract on a minor's behalf that contravenes the Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 (note: a minor under 15 may not work).

13.2 Liability of the Guardian

In certain circumstances the guardian incurs personal contractual liability towards the other contracting party:

  1. If the minor acted as the guardian's agent (the guardian is the principal).
  2. If the guardian guaranteed the minor's performance.
  3. If the guardian bound himself or herself as surety for the minor.

Note: Liability on the basis of negotiorum gestio (unofficial management of another's affairs) is quasi-contractual, not contractual — parents who fail to support their children may be liable on this basis when a third party steps in to support the child.

13.3 Ratification

A minor's unassisted contract creates a natural obligation (not enforceable, but valid enough to be ratified). Ratification converts this into a civil obligation — fully enforceable against both parties with retroactive effect.

  • Ratification may be express or tacit (inferred from conduct).
  • Upon reaching majority, the former minor can ratify their own unassisted contract.
  • A guardian cannot ratify a contract he or she could not validly have concluded on the minor's behalf at the time it was made.
§ CASE
[">Edelstein v Edelstein 1952 (3) SA 1 (A)
FactsA minor concluded an antenuptial contract without the required assistance. After the marriage, the minor (and the guardian) sought to ratify it. IssueCan an antenuptial contract concluded without assistance be ratified after marriage? HeldNo. An antenuptial contract concluded without assistance is void and cannot be ratified by either the minor or the guardian after the marriage has taken place. Why it mattersThis is an absolute rule for antenuptial contracts — once the marriage has occurred, there is no possibility of ratification. The case also confirmed that an unassisted minor's contract can never be valid.

13.4 Statutory Exceptions

Various Acts create exceptions to the rule that a minor cannot incur contractual liability without guardian assistance. Key examples include:

  • Mutual Banks Act — a minor may be a member of a mutual bank and conduct transactions.
  • Banks Act — a minor may open and operate a bank account.
  • Various employment-related statutes (for minors 15 years and older).
ACTIVITY 13.1 — WORKED EXAMPLE

Sipho (17) buys a car without guardian assistance. His guardian later sees it and says "I'm glad you bought it." Does this amount to ratification?

▌ APPROACH

This could amount to tacit ratification. Courts look at all the circumstances to determine whether the guardian intended to ratify. An expression of approval could be sufficient if it clearly signals an intention to be bound. However, ratification requires a clear intention — ambiguous conduct may not suffice. The court would weigh the evidence.

★ EXAM CRITICAL

Always distinguish between: (1) what the guardian can do on behalf of the minor, (2) what the minor can do with guardian consent, and (3) the retroactive effect of ratification. These distinctions frequently appear in problem-type questions.

LEARNING UNIT 14

The Minor's Contractual Capacity — Misrepresentation

What if a minor lies about being an adult in order to get an adult to contract with them? The adult has been deceived — but can they hold the minor liable? This unit explores the basis for liability in such cases.

14.1 The Core Problem

SCENARIO

A minor falsely represents that he/she is a major (or is emancipated, or has the necessary consent). An adult relies on this and enters into a contract. The minor then refuses to perform, relying on minority. The adult is left without a contractual remedy — but is there another remedy?

The generally accepted view: Yes, the minor should be held liable. But on what basis — contract or delict?

14.2 Argument 1 — Contractual Liability (REJECTED)

Three arguments have been advanced in favour of holding the minor contractually liable:

First Argument — Roman-Dutch Writers and Restitutio

Roman-Dutch writers denied the remedy of restitutio in integrum to a fraudulent minor. The argument is: restitutio presupposes a binding contract, therefore there must be a binding contract. This is rejected — restitutio was applied in cases without a binding contract, so its denial does not imply a contract existed.

§ CASE
Louw v MJ & H Trust (Pty) Ltd 1975 (4) SA 268 (T)
FactsA minor misrepresented his age. The court denied him restitutio in integrum and also refused to let him recover the deposit he had already paid. HeldThe court held the minor was not entitled to restitutio. As a result, he could not recover what he had paid. CriticismHeaton and the study guide argue this is wrong: since the minor was not contractually bound, he should have been allowed to recover his deposit via a condictio. The court incorrectly treated him as both bound and not bound. Why it mattersIllustrates the problem with using contractual reasoning in misrepresentation cases. The minor's proper remedy was to recover via condictio (money) or rei vindicatio (property), not restitutio.

Second Argument — Estoppel

Some writers argue the minor is bound by their misrepresentation through the rules of estoppel — they cannot later deny what they represented. Rejected: This would allow the minor to alter their capacity to act through misrepresentation, which is against the policy of protecting minors.

Third Argument — Consumer Protection Act

Section 39(2) of the Consumer Protection Act states the protection for minors (s 39(1)(b)) does not apply if the minor induced the supplier to believe they had full capacity. Rejected as a basis for contractual liability for the same policy reason — the minor would be altering their capacity by committing a misrepresentation.

CONCLUSION — Contractual Liability is UNACCEPTABLE

Holding a fraudulent minor contractually liable would mean the minor can supplement their limited capacity to act through misrepresentation. Capacity to act is limited by law to protect the minor. This protection cannot be circumvented by the minor's own fraud.

14.3 Argument 2 — Delictual Liability (ACCEPTED)

A minor who misrepresents their capacity commits a delict — a wrongful, culpable act causing damage to another. The study guide and Heaton support delictual liability because:

  • It is independent of contractual liability — the contract remains unenforceable.
  • It does not allow the minor to expand their capacity to act.
  • The adult who was prejudiced is protected by having a delictual damages claim.
  • The minor's protection (limited capacity) is preserved.

14.4 Three Requirements for Delictual Liability

REQUIREMENTS — MUST SATISFY ALL THREE
  1. The minor made a misrepresentation regarding majority or capacity to act.
  2. The other party was induced to enter into the contract by the misrepresentation.
  3. The other party suffered damage as a result.

Important principles:

  • The minor must be old enough to be reasonably mistaken for an adult.
  • The other party is entitled to accept the minor's statement of age.
  • The other party is not obliged to investigate unless they have good cause to believe they are dealing with a minor.
  • The onus rests on the minor to prove the other party was not misled.

14.5 Worked Examples (Activity 14.1)

ACTIVITY 14.1 — FOUR WORKED EXAMPLES

Example 1 — Sipho (17): Uses a forged ID to make Mr Molefe believe he is 18. Mr Molefe delivers a car; Sipho refuses to pay.

▌ ANSWER

Sipho IS delictually liable. He made a misrepresentation (forged ID); Mr Molefe was induced to contract; Mr Molefe suffered a loss. No indication Mr Molefe had reason to doubt. All three requirements satisfied.

Example 2 — Justice (17): Claims to be 18 but it is obvious to Mrs Moloi that he cannot be older than 17.

▌ ANSWER

Justice is NOT delictually liable. Mrs Moloi was not induced — she was not misled. She had good cause to believe she was dealing with a minor and was not entitled merely to accept his statement.

Example 3 — Minnie (17): Does not expressly claim to be 18, but knows Mr Ismail thinks she is in her early twenties and says nothing to correct him.

▌ ANSWER

Minnie IS delictually liable. By remaining silent when she knew of his false impression, she made a misrepresentation by conduct. Mr Ismail was induced; he suffered loss.

Example 4 — Maria (17): Contracts with Mr Baloyi, a neighbour who has known her since before she was born.

▌ ANSWER

Maria is NOT delictually liable. Mr Baloyi knew she was a minor. He was not induced by any misrepresentation — he had full knowledge of her age.

EXAM FRAMEWORK — MISREPRESENTATION PROBLEM QUESTIONS

Use this checklist in sequence:

  1. Was there a misrepresentation (active claim, false impression by conduct, or silence creating a false impression)?
  2. Was the other party actually misled/induced? (If they knew the minor's age, no — requirement 2 fails.)
  3. Did the other party suffer damage?
  4. Was the minor old enough to be reasonably mistaken for an adult? (A 10-year-old cannot be.)

Only if all four points are satisfied: the minor is delictually liable. The adult has a damages claim. The contract remains unenforceable.

LEARNING UNIT 15

The Minor's Contractual Capacity — Unjustified Enrichment

Even without a valid contract, and even without misrepresentation, an adult who has already performed in terms of an unassisted contract has one more potential remedy: a claim based on unjustified enrichment.

15.1 What is Unjustified Enrichment?

DEFINITION

A person (A) is unjustifiedly enriched at the expense of another (B) when:

  • A gains a patrimonial benefit (estate value increases), AND
  • at B's expense (B's estate decreases), AND
  • there is no recognised legal ground justifying the transfer.

The law then obliges A to repay B the amount by which A's estate has been and remains enlarged.

In the minor context: adult (B) performs in terms of an unassisted contract; minor (A) receives the benefit but refuses to pay. The minor is enriched at the adult's expense with no legal justification (since the contract is invalid). B can sue A for unjustified enrichment.

§ CASE — KEY AUTHORITY

Edelstein v Edelstein 1952 (3) SA 1 (A)

HeldConfirmed that the contract of a minor acting without assistance can never be valid, even if it is to the minor's benefit. BUT: the minor is still liable if unjustifiedly enriched. ImportanceCapacity to act is not a requirement for liability based on enrichment. The policy reason is fairness — one should not profit at another's expense without justification.

15.2 How is Enrichment Calculated?

This is a critical calculation section — problem-type questions will require you to apply these three rules:

🔢 ENRICHMENT CALCULATION — THREE RULES
Rule 1 — WHEN?
The enrichment is calculated at the moment the other party institutes the claim (not the date of contracting).
Rule 2 — HOW MUCH?
Calculate two amounts: (a) how much the minor's estate increased (actual value of what the minor received), and (b) how much the other party's estate decreased (actual value they parted with). The contract price is ignored. The minor is liable for the lesser of the two amounts.
Rule 3 — CHANGED CIRCUMSTANCES
  • Asset lost or stolen → other party gets nothing.
  • Asset sold, proceeds spent on luxuries → minor liable only for what remains of the proceeds.
  • Asset sold, proceeds used on necessaries (food, clothing, accommodation, medical treatment) → minor liable for the cost of the necessaries, even if nothing remains. (Reason: the minor would have had to pay for these anyway — the enrichment equals the necessaries cost.)
  • Asset damaged → minor liable for the current reduced value at the date the claim is brought.
ACTIVITY 15 — WORKED CALCULATION

Peter (16) buys a motorbike for R2 000 from Dan (adult), unassisted. At contracting, the bike is worth R1 500. Dan delivers. Peter refuses to pay. On 3 March Peter sells the bike for R1 000 — spends R500 on food/clothes (necessaries) and R500 on a holiday (luxury). On 20 March Dan sues.

▌ ANSWER

(a) Amount Peter is liable for (claim on 20 March, after selling):
Proceeds: R1 000. R500 spent on necessaries → counts as enrichment (R500). R500 on holiday → luxury, nothing remains → R0. Total enrichment = R500. Dan's estate decreased by R1 500 (actual value). Lesser = R500.

(b) If Peter sold on 30 March (after claim instituted on 20 March):
At 20 March the bike was still in Peter's possession, worth R1 500 (original value if undamaged and still held). Peter's enrichment = R1 500. Dan's loss = R1 500. Lesser = R1 500.

(c) If bike damaged, worth R800 on claim date:
Enrichment = R800 (current value). Dan's loss = R1 500. Lesser = R800.

(d) If bike stolen before claim:
Asset lost → R0. Dan gets nothing.

DEEP DIVE — CONTRACT vs. ENRICHMENT LIABILITY

This distinction is huge in problem questions. If the minor is contractually liable (e.g. after ratification), they owe the contract price regardless of when the claim is brought. Under unjustified enrichment, the minor only owes the current value of remaining enrichment — which can be far less. A motorbike worth R50 000 at contracting might be worth only R20 000 when the claim is brought — that is all the minor pays under enrichment.

LEARNING UNIT 16

The Minor's Contractual Capacity — Restitutio in Integrum

Critical distinction from Units 14 & 15: Those units dealt with an invalid contract (no guardian assistance). Restitutio in integrum only applies where the contract is valid.

16.1 What is Restitutio in Integrum?

⚖ DEFINITION

An extraordinary legal remedy that allows a minor to escape contractual liability even under a valid contract. Both parties are placed in the position they would have been in if the contract had never been entered into. All performances are reversed.

16.2 Two Requirements

  1. Valid contract — the minor must have contracted with the guardian's assistance, or the guardian must have ratified the contract, or the court must have given consent. (Restitutio is also available even if the court consented — because the court might have erred or been misled.)
  2. Prejudice to the minor — the contract must be to the minor's disadvantage, or the minor must suffer some prejudice as a result.

16.3 Effect of Restitutio

When granted, restitutio operates retroactively — the contract is treated as if it never existed. Each party must return what they received. This is a far-reaching remedy and courts will only grant it where the circumstances genuinely warrant it.

ACTIVITY 16.1 — WORKED EXAMPLE

Thomas (17, minor) sees a laptop advertised for R5 000. His guardian signs the contract on his behalf. Later, Thomas realises the laptop is actually only worth R2 000 on the market. Can he rely on restitutio?

▌ ANSWER

There is a valid contract (guardian assistance). The contract is prejudicial to Thomas (he is overpaying by R3 000). Both requirements are satisfied — Thomas may apply for restitutio in integrum. If granted, both parties are restored: Thomas returns the laptop, the other party returns the R5 000 (or the deposit paid).

LEARNING UNIT 17

The Minor's Capacity in Respect of Other Juristic Acts, Litigation & Delictual and Criminal Liability

This unit moves beyond contracts to look at the minor's capacity across all other areas of legal life.

17.1 Other Agreements

The same rules for contractual capacity apply to other agreements:

  • A minor can enter into an agreement that only confers rights on the minor (unilateral benefit) without guardian assistance.
  • A minor cannot enter into an agreement that extinguishes the minor's debt (releases another from what they owe the minor) without guardian assistance — this diminishes the minor's estate.
  • A minor cannot independently enter into a real agreement (which transfers ownership of property) — the same capacity rules apply.

17.2 Wills

  • A minor under 16 cannot make a will.
  • A minor 16 or older may make a valid will independently.
  • A minor may witness a will only if 14 years or older.

17.3 Marriage and Civil Unions

Minors generally require parental/guardian consent to marry. In certain cases, additional consent is needed from the Minister of Home Affairs or the Commissioner of Child Welfare (or the High Court). Important principles:

  • A void marriage does not terminate minority.
  • An annulled voidable marriage restores the minor's limited capacity with retroactive effect.
  • Civil unions under the Civil Union Act are restricted to persons already 18 — a minor cannot acquire majority by entering a civil union.

17.4 Medical Treatment and Operations

17.5 Holding Offices and Performing Functions

A minor generally cannot hold public offices or perform certain official functions. The limitations exist because such positions carry responsibilities that require full legal capacity. Heaton sets out specific limitations.

17.6 Capacity to Litigate

GENERAL RULE

A minor generally cannot litigate without the assistance of his or her guardian. The guardian must assist in all civil proceedings involving the minor.

Exception — Venia Agendi: The High Court (as upper guardian of all minors) may grant a minor venia agendi — permission to conduct a suit independently, without guardian assistance. This is granted only if the minor has reached a relatively advanced level of intellectual development.

Criminal proceedings: A minor may be charged criminally without guardian assistance, but the parent/guardian must be present at proceedings if the child is under 18.

17.7 Delictual and Criminal Liability — Accountability

⚖ KEY DISTINCTION: Capacity to Act vs. Accountability

Capacity to act = the ability to perform valid juristic acts (requires understanding nature, extent and consequences of acts).

Accountability = the capacity to distinguish between right and wrong and to act accordingly. This is required for both delictual and criminal liability.

A minor may be accountable even though lacking capacity to act — they are different legal concepts.

LEARNING UNIT 18

Termination of Minority — Attainment of the Prescribed Age, Marriage, Venia Aetatis & Release from Tutelage

Minority ends when one of the prescribed events occurs. This unit deals with three of those events.

18.1 Attainment of the Prescribed Age

⚖ KEY LAW

The Children's Act 38 of 2005 lowered the age of majority from 21 (under the former Age of Majority Act 57 of 1972) to 18 years for all children in South Africa, with effect from 1 July 2007.

Majority ensues at the beginning of the day of the person's 18th birthday.

Important: The Children's Act does not have retroactive effect.

§ CASE

Malcolm v Premier, Western Cape Government NO 2014 (3) SA 177 (SCA)

FactsMalcolm was born in June 1987. He contracted Hepatitis B at age 6 in 1993. The question was when prescription of his claim ran from. IssueDid the lowering of the age of majority by the Children's Act affect prescriptions that were running before 1 July 2007? HeldNo. The Children's Act has no retroactive effect. Malcolm's prescription ran from one year after he turned 21 (not 18), since the Children's Act was not yet in operation when he was born. Key DistinctionIf Malcolm had been born in June 2008 (after the Act came into operation), prescription would run from one year after he turned 18.

Customary Law

In customary law there is no fixed age at which majority is attained. Majority depends on custom and tradition within the specific customary community.

Prescription and Minors

Normally, prescription of claims runs three years from when the claim arose. For minors, prescription is postponed until one year after the minor becomes a major. This is a crucial protective rule.

18.2 Marriage or Civil Union

  • A person who enters into a valid civil or customary marriage before turning 18 becomes a major for all purposes.
  • If the marriage is dissolved by death or divorce before 18, minority does not revive.
  • A void marriage does not terminate minority.
  • Annulment of a voidable marriage restores the minor's limited capacity with retroactive effect.
  • A civil union under the Civil Union Act is restricted to persons already 18 — a minor cannot acquire majority this way.
ACTIVITY 18 — MARRIAGE SCENARIOS

Nomsa (17) marries Sipho in a valid customary marriage. Six months later Sipho dies. Is Nomsa a major or a minor?

▌ ANSWER

Nomsa became a major upon the valid customary marriage. When Sipho died, her minority did not revive — once terminated by a valid marriage, minority is permanently ended regardless of the marriage's subsequent dissolution.

LEARNING UNIT 19

Termination of Minority — Emancipation

Emancipation is the freedom to enter into contracts independently, granted to a minor by a parent or guardian. This does not make the minor a major in all respects — it is a more limited concept.

19.1 What is Emancipation?

⚖ DEFINITION

Emancipation occurs where a guardian grants a minor the freedom to enter into contracts independently. The minor acts without needing guardian assistance for those contracts.

It does not extend to alienating or encumbering immovable property, or to contracting marriage.

19.2 Forms of Emancipation

Express (Historical) Emancipation

Originally a declaration before the court by the parent/guardian that they had emancipated their child from parental authority. This was later replaced by venia aetatis and is no longer used in this form.

Implied (Tacit) Emancipation

This is the modern and relevant form. Implied emancipation occurs when a minor lives apart from their parents and manages their own undertaking. It can itself be express or tacit — the question is always one of fact.

19.3 Factors Courts Consider

Whether emancipation has occurred is a question of fact. Courts look at:

  • Whether the minor lives on their own and manages their own business.
  • The minor's age.
  • The relationship between the minor and the guardian.
  • The nature of the minor's occupation.
  • The length of time the occupation has been carried on.

19.4 Extent of Emancipation

The extent of emancipation is also a question of fact:

  • General emancipation — covers all contracts (not just those related to the minor's trade). Must be clearly proved. Excludes: alienation/encumbrance of immovable property and marriage.
  • Partial emancipation — limited to contracts connected with the minor's business or occupation. If the guardian has only emancipated a minor for their business, capacity is restricted to that scope.

19.6 Revocation of Emancipation

A guardian may revoke emancipation, but only to the extent that third parties acting in good faith have not already relied on it. Revocation does not affect rights already acquired by third parties.

19.7 Burden of Proof

The onus of proving tacit emancipation rests on the person who alleges that emancipation took place.

✎ ACTIVITY 19 — WORKED EXAMPLE

Zanele (17) has been running her own hair salon for 18 months, lives in her own flat, and manages her own finances with her mother's full knowledge and approval. She signs a lease for new salon equipment worth R40 000 without consulting her mother. Can she be held liable?

▌ ANSWER

This is a strong case for implied emancipation. The factors point strongly toward emancipation: she lives independently, runs her own business, manages her own affairs, and her guardian knows and approves. The lease relates to her business — so even if only partially emancipated for business matters, this falls within scope. She is very likely to be held liable on the contract as an emancipated minor. The onus of proving emancipation lies on whoever asserts it (the lessor, here).

Diverse Factors Which Affect Status

This final unit examines how various conditions beyond age can affect a person's status — specifically their legal capacity, capacity to act, capacity to litigate, and accountability.

20.1 Status — Quick Recap

Legal CapacityBeing a legal subject — holding rights and duties. All persons have this.
Capacity to ActAbility to perform valid juristic acts. Can be limited or absent.
Capacity to LitigateLocus standi in iudicio — ability to appear in court as a party.
AccountabilityCapacity to be held responsible for crimes and delicts.

20.2 Mental Illness

Mental Illness

Definition

The Mental Health Care Act 17 of 2002 defines a mental illness as a positive diagnosis of a mental health condition in terms of accepted diagnostic criteria (DSM-5 or ICD). Common-law tests also apply — the question is whether the person can understand the nature, purport and consequences of their acts.

Legal Status of a Mentally Ill Person

⚖ EFFECTS
  • Capacity to act: None. All juristic acts are void (not voidable) — the law attaches no consequences to the will of a mentally ill person.
  • Capacity to litigate: None — cannot appear as a party in their own right.
  • Lucidum Intervallum (lucid interval): If the person performs a juristic act during a genuine lucid interval, the act is valid. The critical moment is when the act is performed.
  • Certification does NOT determine capacity: A certified person who is lucid at the moment of acting has capacity; an uncertified person who is mentally ill at the moment has no capacity. The factual state at the specific moment governs.

Curators for Mentally Ill Persons

Curator TypeFunction
Curator BonisAdministers the mentally ill person's property/estate. Appointment does not automatically deprive the person of all capacity to act — a lucid person can still act validly.
Curator PersonaeLooks after the mentally ill person's personal and health affairs where they cannot manage these.
Curator ad LitemAssists the mentally ill person in litigation.

20.3 Inability to Manage One's Own Affairs

⚖ KEY POINTS

The court may appoint a curator bonis for any person who, because of a physical or mental disability or incapacity (not necessarily mental illness), cannot manage their own affairs. Examples: deaf-mute, blind, senile, paralysed, seriously ill.

Crucially: appointment of a curator does not result in total loss of capacity to act. Each situation must be assessed at the specific moment of the juristic act. If the person was capable of managing their affairs at that moment, the act is valid.

20.4 Influence of Alcohol and Drugs

⚖ KEY PRINCIPLES
  • "Intoxication" covers the effect of any drug, not only alcohol.
  • The test is high: the person must be so affected that they do not know what they are doing or the consequences of their acts. Mere increased willingness or persuadability is insufficient.
  • If this threshold is met: the juristic act is void (not voidable).
  • Effect is temporary — only lasts as long as the intoxication persists.
  • The person who alleges intoxication must prove it.

20.5 Prodigality

Definition

⚖ DEFINITION

A prodigal is a person who has normal mental ability but cannot manage their own affairs because they squander their assets in an irresponsible and reckless way, due to some defect in their powers of judgement or character. A prodigal is thus different from a mentally ill person — their intellect is intact, but their financial judgement is fundamentally defective.

Phil Morkel Bpk v Niemand
1970 (3) SA 455 (C)
Held

A prodigal's capacity to act is curtailed because they lack the necessary judgement to know which obligations they should be party to — not because they cannot take part normally in creating an obligation. The reason for protection is the same as for minors: lack of judgement (onoordeelkundigheid).

Why it matters: Distinguishes the prodigal's restriction (lack of judgement, like a minor) from a mentally ill person's (inability to understand). Prodigality = court-imposed restriction; mental illness = inherent incapacity.

Declaring a Prodigal — Interdiction

A court declares a person a prodigal through an order called interdiction. After interdiction, the prodigal's status changes significantly.

Legal CapacityRemains — a prodigal is still a legal subject with rights and duties.
Capacity to ActSeverely limited — the interdicted prodigal generally cannot incur obligations or alienate property independently.
Capacity to LitigateLimited — generally cannot litigate independently; requires curator assistance.
AccountabilityNot affected — a prodigal remains fully accountable for crimes and delicts.

A curator bonis is appointed to administer the prodigal's estate.

Constitutional Implications

Interdiction as a prodigal potentially infringes on constitutional rights (autonomy, dignity). The courts have had to grapple with whether such interdiction is constitutionally justifiable. The relevant discussion is in Heaton — know that there are constitutional concerns about the extent to which a prodigal's rights are curtailed.

20.6 Insolvency

Definition

An insolvent is a person whose liabilities exceed their assets, and whose estate has been sequestrated (formally declared insolvent) by the High Court.

Legal CapacitySurvives — the insolvent remains a legal subject. Certain rights are restricted by statute (e.g. cannot serve in certain offices).
Capacity to ActPartially limited — may still conclude certain contracts independently; others require trustee consent. Contracts in breach are voidable by the trustee.
Capacity to LitigatePartially suspended — civil proceedings stayed until trustee appointed. After trustee appointment, the insolvent retains capacity to litigate in personal matters.
AccountabilityFully retained — insolvency does not affect accountability for crimes or delicts.

What Happens to the Estate?

  • Upon sequestration, the estate vests in the Master of the High Court until a trustee is appointed.
  • Once the trustee is appointed, the insolvent estate vests in the trustee.
  • The insolvent can still acquire assets after sequestration — these are generally protected from creditors who were owed money before sequestration.
  • If the insolvent commits a delict after sequestration, compensation is paid from post-sequestration assets.

Contracts After Sequestration

The insolvent may still conclude certain contracts independently (relating to personal matters and post-sequestration property). Contracts affecting the insolvent estate typically require the trustee's consent. If concluded in breach of this requirement, they are voidable at the trustee's election.

  • Thinking certification = no capacity. Wrong — the factual state at the moment of the act governs.
  • Thinking insolvency removes all capacity to litigate. Wrong — only certain civil proceedings are stayed pending trustee appointment.
  • Forgetting that prodigals and insolvents retain full accountability for crimes and delicts.

Master Summary — Remedies for Adult Contracting with Unassisted Minor

When an adult has contracted with an unassisted minor (invalid contract) and already performed, they have up to three potential remedies:

RemedyTriggerWhat the Adult GetsUnit
Delictual Damages (Misrepresentation)Minor misrepresented their age/capacity AND adult was induced AND suffered lossDamages to put adult in position they would have been in without the misrepresentation14
Unjustified EnrichmentMinor was enriched at adult's expense without legal groundAmount by which minor's estate was CURRENTLY enriched (lesser of two values)15
Restitutio in IntegrumContract is VALID (assisted) but prejudicial to the minor — minor seeks this remedyFull reversal of performances (minor's remedy against valid contract)16

Key rule: Restitutio is the minor's remedy. Misrepresentation and enrichment are the adult's remedies.
